## **STEP 3 — STUDY THE CREATOR'S VOICE**


Read the reference transcript carefully. You are not looking for facts. You are looking for the person.

Document:

**A. How they actually build sentences.** Does speech run in long chains joined by "and," "like," "because," "so"? Or short punchy statements? Where do they actually pause mid-thought? Where do they trail off and restart? Can the first sentence of any paragraph be planned before the speaker opens their mouth — or does it build clause by clause as the speaker discovers where it's going?

**B. How they handle moral or opinion statements.** Do they state positions plainly and move on, or hedge heavily? Do they preface opinions with "I think" / "I feel" / "I don't know, maybe I'm wrong"? Do they concede opponents' good points before criticising?

**C. Their verbal fingerprint.** Specific words, phrases, comparisons, registers that are native to this person. Gaming vocabulary? Internet vocabulary? Academic vocabulary? Physical or sensory observations? Self-deprecating tangents? How do they address their audience — "chat," "guys," "you," something else?

**D. How they begin and end thoughts.** Do they front-load their conclusion or build to it? How do they actually close a topic — tidy summary, or just stop when they run out of things to say? Do they use paragraph-ending thesis statements or do thoughts just run into the next one?

**E. What they never do.** Identify rhetorical devices this person does NOT use. These are as important as what they do use.

**F. What personal experiences and credentials they actually have.** Document specifically what the creator can and cannot claim firsthand authority over. What have they personally done, worked on, witnessed, or been part of? This list determines what can be written in first person with genuine authority. Anything outside this list must never be presented as the creator's own experience.

**G. Their personality-to-argument ratio.** Identify where in the reference transcript the creator's personal voice breaks through — moments of genuine reaction, humor, self-expression — versus where they are purely making an argument. Note the approximate ratio and identify the specific types of moments that bring personality into the content. This ratio must be maintained in the script.

This persona study must be specific enough that you could write a paragraph and a reader familiar with the creator would recognise it as authentic. General adjectives like "casual" or "authentic" are not enough.
