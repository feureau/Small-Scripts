We've learned a method for answering criticism that mistakes a work's genre DNA for a flaw — and at the heart of that method sits one perfect story.

---

## The James Cameron Story

During the making of 2009's *Avatar*, 20th Century Fox executives pushed back on the film's extended Mountain Banshee flight sequences. The scene in question follows protagonist Jake Sully learning to ride the winged creature and taking it out for a soaring ride through Pandora's landscape. It's lengthy. It's visually spectacular. And, as the studio noted, it doesn't move the plot forward.

As Cameron tells it, the executives kept saying things like, "Well you can cut out all that flying stuff, we don't need all that. That doesn't advance the plot." Cameron's response has become legendary:

> "You're absolutely correct, it doesn't advance the plot. It's doing something completely else. It's allowing people to enjoy the moment."

But the story doesn't end there. Cameron also deployed what might be the ultimate filmmaker flex. He reminded the executives who paid for the building they were sitting in:

> "You know what? I made *Titanic*. This building that we're meeting in right now, this new half-billion dollar complex on your lot? *Titanic* paid for that, so I get to do this."

And afterward, they thanked him. As Cameron put it: "I feel that my job is to protect their investment, often against their own judgment. But as long as I protect their investment, all is forgiven."

He was right. Exit polling and data gathering later confirmed that the flying sequences were what audiences loved most.

---

### The Larger Philosophy

In the years since, Cameron has expanded on this philosophy. During his MasterClass, he articulated why "rules" about what belongs in a film don't always apply:

> "There are a lot of rules and advisories about why you put things in movies, and that they should all serve a purpose. Except, they don't. Sometimes it should just be something you want to see as a filmmaker… and sometimes the only way to see it is to show it."

And he defined the real purpose of such scenes:

> "If I want to see it, there are lots of people who are going to want to see it. And they want to see it for itself, not because of a purpose. The purpose is to be present; to be in that world."

Speaking about the "hangout" sequences in *Avatar: The Way of Water*, Cameron went further, contrasting his earnest approach with the prevailing tone of modern blockbusters:

> "People forget to put beauty into a film. There's a lot of snark, there's a lot of sarcasm, there's a lot of cutesy jokes in movies. There's a lot of people playing things off as if they're super cool and therefore diluting the sense of stakes, the sense of jeopardy. I go straight at just being earnest. If there's jeopardy, it's real. People could die. And if you like what you see, let's just hang out for a bit. Let's not rush through this because of artificial concepts like 'plot.'"

And he named the core tension between two different ways of judging a film:

> "Ultimately you have to kind of arbitrate between the conventional ruleset and the experiential ruleset."

---

### Why This Story Matters for Our Discussion

Cameron's story isn't just a fun anecdote — it's the perfect lens for understanding the criticism leveled at *The Mandalorian & Grogu*. The critics who say it "lacks character development," feels "inconsequential," or "doesn't justify its existence as a movie" are applying what Cameron calls the **conventional ruleset**: every scene must advance the plot, every beat must build character, every film must justify its format. Those aren't invalid standards — they're just the wrong ones for a story built in the pulp serial tradition, where the *experiential ruleset* governs: let's hang out in this world, let's watch these characters we love just *be*, let's enjoy the moment.

Cameron understood that "the purpose is to be present; to be in that world." That's what a Saturday-afternoon serial offers, and that's what *The Mandalorian & Grogu* — a compressed season of television on the big screen — is offering too. The criticism isn't wrong about what's on screen. It's just using the wrong ruleset to judge it.

---

### What We've Learned: The Full Method

1. **Identify the unspoken expectation behind the criticism.** Every critique carries a hidden assumption about what a "good" film should do. "Lack of character development" assumes growth is the goal. "No stakes" assumes a story must threaten the galaxy to matter. Name that assumption — then ask whether it's the right yardstick for this kind of story.

2. **Reframe the "flaw" as a feature of the intended tradition.** Don't deny the observation — explain why it *belongs*. Mando getting knocked out isn't lazy writing; it's the cliffhanger rhythm of a serial. The "stupid" Hutt twins aren't incompetent villains; they're theatrical archetypes whose arrogance creates the hero's escape hatch. The helmet returning isn't a plot hole; it's the serial logic of an iconic prop.

3. **Use the Cameron structure — "You're right, it doesn't X. It's doing Y."** This is the master move: acknowledge the critic's observation, concede its accuracy on its own terms, then completely redefine what the scene or story is trying to accomplish. It's not defensive. It's not argumentative. It's just a confident re-framing.

4. **Anchor the defense in the work's own DNA.** Cameron could point to *Titanic* and say "I know what audiences want." We can point to George Lucas's love of *Flash Gordon* serials and the fact that *The Mandalorian & Grogu* began life as a season of television. The episodic feel isn't a bug — it's the format showing through. Criticising it for being what it is would be like criticising a haiku for being short.

5. **Name the two rulesets.** Cameron distinguished between the "conventional ruleset" (plot, momentum, efficiency) and the "experiential ruleset" (presence, beauty, hanging out). The criticism comes from applying the conventional ruleset. The defence comes from recognising that the experiential ruleset is also valid — and sometimes, it's the one that audiences actually love most.

6. **Treat the critic's experience as valid, but incomplete.** If someone found it dull or inconsequential, that's real. The method isn't about saying "you're wrong to feel that." It's about saying "what you felt was the natural result of a mismatch between expectations and the film's actual genre. Here's the other way to experience it." Cameron said it best: "It's funny, the scenes they complain about being too long are the same scenes they love. It's just not the same people that are complaining and loving it."

---

In the end, Cameron's wisdom — born in a studio boardroom, proven at the box office, and refined across decades — gives us the clearest way to answer any criticism that confuses a genre choice for a flaw. Sometimes a scene isn't there to advance the plot. Sometimes a character isn't there to change. Sometimes a movie isn't there to be an event.

Sometimes it's just there to let you enjoy the moment.
